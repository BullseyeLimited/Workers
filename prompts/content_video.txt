Video vs Photo – Key Differences in Metadata

Describing video content is more complex than describing a photo. A video isn’t a single frozen moment – it’s a sequence of moments over time, complete with movement, sound, and evolving visuals. Here are the key differences and how we adapt the metadata framework:
	•	Time-Based Changes: Unlike a photo, a video has a timeline. The subject’s actions, poses, and expressions change over time, and the environment or camera may shift. The metadata must capture this progression. We do this via a timestamped Long Description, breaking the video into segments (with mm:ss timestamps) that describe significant actions and changes as they happen. This gives a play-by-play that a reasoning AI can follow.
	•	Audio and Speech: Videos include audio, which could be the creator’s voice, background music, moans, laughter, or other sounds. We introduce a Voice Transcript field to capture all spoken words verbatim, as well as notable non-verbal sounds. This is critical: the persona AI will “know” exactly what was said or heard in the video and can respond or refer to it. For example, if the creator whispers “I wish you were here,” the AI can later respond to that sentiment. Include vocal tone or inflection in brackets (e.g., whispering, laughing) so the AI can gauge mood and intensity from the audio.
	•	Dynamic Visual Detail: A video may start with one scene or outfit and end with another (e.g., she removes clothing or moves from one room to another). The metadata needs to reflect transitions: if she takes off her robe at 00:30, or if the lighting goes from daylight to candlelight mid-video, note those changes at the appropriate timestamp. The Long Description should read like a detailed storyboard or shot-by-shot breakdown.
	•	Continuity & Reasoning: Because videos have a narrative flow, the metadata helps the AI maintain continuity in chat. For instance, if in the video the creator starts shy and later becomes confident, the mood tags and description should reflect that progression. The AI might then adjust its responses as the video progresses (if it’s “live”) or after the fact (“You started so shy but ended up so bold in that clip!”). Therefore, we must tag mood and actions comprehensively over the video’s span, not just the beginning.
	•	Increased Detail Volume: Expect the Long Description and Voice Transcript to be much longer than for a photo description. That’s normal. It’s better to err on the side of more detail so the AI has full awareness. However, do not add any information that isn’t actually present in the video. Stay factual: no guessing what she’s thinking, no flowery metaphors about the “vibe.” Describe what you see and hear, not what isn’t explicit. For example, if she smiles, describe the smile (warm, playful, etc.), but don’t say “she feels happy” (feeling is inferred, not seen).
	•	Technical Formatting: We use the same field names and format as the photo metadata, but adapted for video. Notably, we add Duration Seconds (video length in seconds) and a filled Voice Transcript (instead of leaving it blank as for photos). The Long Description will be structured into timestamped lines instead of one paragraph. All other fields (title, tags, etc.) remain and should be filled out in a manner consistent with the video content.

General Approach to Describing Videos

When describing a video, imagine you are painting a word-picture for someone who cannot see or hear it. You are effectively transcribing the visual and auditory experience into text. Stay factual to what is observable (sights and sounds), but also be evocative and precise enough to convey mood and significance. Keep all descriptions in third person (e.g., “She slowly runs her fingers through her hair as she gazes into the camera…”). These metadata descriptions are used behind the scenes; if the AI persona needs to narrate in first person later, it will convert appropriately. Do not write from the creator’s first-person perspective, and do not address the viewer as “you.”

Tone and style: Use clear, straightforward language. For sensual or erotic content, the tone should be seductive and tastefully descriptive, not crude or overly clinical . Even for very explicit NSFW videos, avoid pornographic or vulgar terms when a polite word will do – describe actions and body parts respectfully and vividly, but don’t use profanity or slurs. Every word should help the next AI understand and convey the video accurately. Use present tense and active voice for immediacy (e.g., “She is kneeling on the bed, arching her back…” rather than “She was kneeling” or “has knelt”) .

No abstract or metaphorical language. Do not use poetic comparisons or figurative language that isn’t literally shown. For example, don’t write “her eyes sparkle like stars” or “moving with feline grace,” which are subjective or flowery. Stick to concrete visuals and sounds: e.g., “her eyes catch the soft light” or “she arches her body gracefully” – factual and specific. Avoid filler words and generic phrases that don’t add information. If you mention mood or vibe, tie it to something tangible in the video (e.g., “she smiles playfully while stripping off her bra” conveys a playful mood through action). Everything should be useful for an AI’s reasoning. The description is not for poetic effect; it’s to enable understanding (e.g., identifying that she was smiling indicates a flirty mood).

Level of detail: Be comprehensive. Describe every significant action, pose, and expression the creator does, especially anything the camera focuses on. Small details that might affect an AI’s response (a wink, a giggle, a sudden change in pacing) should be noted. However, you don’t need to describe trivial movements that have no bearing on context (e.g., if she scratches her nose briefly unless it’s meant to be cute or relevant). Use your judgment on what a downstream AI would care about: err toward inclusion if uncertain.

Background elements: Mention the setting and any important background details (is it in a kitchen, a bedroom, a balcony with city lights?). Include background sounds if they set context – e.g., faint music, traffic noise, a TV playing in the background – but do not over-prioritize background audio or scenery at the expense of the creator’s actions. The focus should remain on the creator and their performance. For example, note “soft music playing in background” if she’s dancing to it, but if there’s a distant car honk once, you can ignore it unless it startles her or changes her behavior. The environment and ambience should be captured just enough to give context and flavor.

Consistency: Ensure that all fields you provide tell a coherent story without contradictions. The Long Description, tags, and transcript should all align. For instance, if the Long Description says she takes off her red lingerie, then Outfit Layers should include that red lingerie (with a note it’s removed), and Explicitness should reflect that nudity occurs. If the Voice Transcript shows she said “It’s so hot in here,” perhaps the Mood Tags include “playful” or “sensual” depending on how she said it. Cross-check that nothing you write in one field is negated by another field.

Metadata Fields and Guidelines

Title

Purpose: A brief, catchy name for the video. Think of it like a caption or label that allows one to identify the clip at a glance. The title should capture the essence or highlight of the video in just a few words.

Length & Format: Very short (typically 2-5 words, up to ~40 characters). Use Title Case (capitalize main words, minor words like “and” or “of” can be lowercase). No period at the end. The title is not a sentence, just a phrase.

Content: Include a key detail or theme from the video – often combining an element of time/setting + action/outfit/mood. For example, if the video is a morning routine in lingerie, a good title might be “Morning Lingerie Tease.” If it’s an outdoor vlog-style clip on a balcony, maybe “Sunrise Balcony Hello.” Avoid generic or non-descriptive titles like “My video” or “Clip 123.” Make it unique enough to distinguish the scene at a glance .

Tone: You can be slightly creative or evocative, but remain grounded in what’s actually in the video. The title should entice while staying factual. For instance, a video of a bubble bath could be titled “Midnight Bubble Bath” – it’s intriguing but still literally about a bubble bath at night. Do not use explicit or vulgar language in titles, even for NSFW videos. Allude to sexual content in a classy or playful way if needed (e.g., “Steamy Bath Play” instead of “Masturbating in Bath”) . The title is often user-facing, so keep it relatively tame.

Examples:
	•	Good: “Morning Bed Tease” – (implies a morning scenario on a bed that is teasing or playful)
	•	Good: “Lace Lingerie Stretch” – (if the video features a stretching routine in lingerie; we get outfit and action)
	•	Bad: “sexy video lol” – (too informal, vague, and unprofessional)
	•	Bad: “Video 001” – (generic and not descriptive at all)

Make sure the Title gives a hint of the scenario or mood without being a full sentence. It should be distinct and memorable, but still directly reflective of the video content.

Short Description

Purpose: A one-liner or very brief summary of the video. This will often be shown as a caption or teaser under the video in chat or content feeds. It should highlight the most important elements – the subject (usually “she”), what she’s doing, where, and perhaps the mood – in a single sentence (or two short sentences).

Length: ~15-30 words in one sentence if possible (two short sentences max) . It must be concise enough to read at a glance.

Content: Mention the subject (implicitly the creator, usually as “she”) and her main action or pose, along with any especially eye-catching detail like a piece of outfit or the setting . You can subtly convey mood or intention here with a descriptor (e.g., “playfully,” “slowly,” “seductively”), but don’t cram in too many specifics – save detailed sequence for the Long Description. The short description should answer: “What’s happening overall in this video?” For example: “She stands on her balcony in a silk robe, smiling and waving good morning.” Or for a spicier clip: “She slowly peels off her lace panties on the bed, throwing a playful wink at the camera.” We get the picture in one line.

Style: It should be engaging and descriptive, possibly with a teasing tone for flirty content . But it must remain accurate to the video – do not introduce any action or detail that isn’t actually visible. Imagine this line as the text that would make a fan want to watch the video, while still being a truthful summary.

Perspective: Third person, present tense, and do not address the viewer as “you.” For example, say “She arches her back against the window, laughing softly as sunlight fills the room,” rather than “I arch my back…” or “You see her arching her back…” The short description is an objective narrator’s view of the video’s highlight moment .

Examples:
	•	Good: “Wrapped in a fluffy towel, she steps onto the balcony and sips her coffee with a bright smile.” – (We get subject “she,” setting on balcony in a towel, action sipping coffee, mood bright morning happiness, all in one sentence.)
	•	Good: “She does a slow, sultry stretch on the bed in red lingerie, arching her back and biting her lip.” – (Subject “she,” action stretching on bed, outfit red lingerie, mood sultry/flirty via “biting her lip.” It’s concise yet vivid.)
	•	Bad: “This video shows her being sexy on a bed. It is very seductive and hot.” – (Too generic and also breaks form by commenting “very seductive and hot” without specifics. Also, it’s two sentences that add little detail. We want specifics, not just adjectives like “sexy.”)
	•	Bad: “She looks at you and wants you so much.” – (Addresses the viewer and assumes intentions that aren’t directly observed; also too explicit in a non-descriptive way.)

In summary, the Short Description should tease the content of the video in a factual, enticing manner, encapsulating the core of the scene in one smooth sentence.

Long Description

Purpose: A detailed, timestamped breakdown of the video’s visuals and actions from start to finish. This is the heart of the video metadata. It should be so clear and thorough that someone (or some AI) who hasn’t seen the video could reconstruct each moment in their mind. The Long Description for video is essentially a scene-by-scene narration with time markers.

Format: Break the description into segments keyed by timestamps. Use the MM:SS format (minutes:seconds) for timestamps (e.g., 00:00 for the start, 00:05, 00:15, 01:00 etc.). Each segment begins with a timestamp, followed by an en dash or hyphen and then the description of what is happening at that time. For example:

00:00 – She waves at the camera with a cheerful smile, standing by the railing in her silk robe.  
00:10 – The camera pans down slowly as she opens her robe, revealing black lace lingerie underneath...  

and so on. Use a new line for each timestamped segment. You do not need to put every single second; use timestamps at meaningful change points. Typically, update the description every 5–30 seconds, depending on how fast things change. Short videos (~15s) might have just 2–3 timestamps (start, middle, end). Longer videos (1–2 minutes) might have a dozen timestamps. The key is to mark any moment where the scene, action, or position noticeably shifts .
	•	Start at 00:00: Always begin at 00:00 with a description of the opening frame or initial action. Describe the setting and her starting pose/attire here as needed (e.g., “00:00 – She is lying on her bed in red lingerie, propped up on her elbows and grinning at the camera.”).
	•	Progression: With each new timestamp, describe what’s happening from that point onward. This can be narrative: think of it like “storyboarding” the video. Mention movements, expressions, gestures, and key “beats” of action. For instance, if at 00:10 she starts to unclasp her bra, capture that: “00:10 – She reaches behind her back and unhooks her bra, teasingly sliding the straps off her shoulders.” If by 00:20 the bra is off and she’s doing something else, note that at 00:20.
	•	Level of detail: Be very detailed. Describe physical actions (e.g., stands up, sits down, walks closer, runs her fingers through her hair), facial expressions (smiling, biting her lip, making eye contact with a sultry gaze, eyes closed in pleasure, etc.), body language (arch of back, spreading legs, tossing hair), and any interactions (if there are objects like a toy, a phone, etc., describe how she uses them). If the camera cuts to a different angle or there’s a significant zoom, mention it (“camera zooms in on her face as she whispers…”). Essentially, capture what the viewer sees at each moment and how it changes.
	•	Outfit changes or removal: If during the video she removes or changes clothing, note the exact time and what comes off or on. For example, “00:45 – She slips the robe off her shoulders, letting it drop to the floor, now standing in just her lingerie.” If the outfit change is a major event, you might also mention the state after (“…now fully nude” etc.). This ties into outfit metadata fields as well, but the Long Description provides the timeline of the striptease or change.
	•	Background and setting details: Include descriptions of the environment as relevant, especially if it changes. For instance, if she moves from the bedroom to the bathroom in the video, mark that (“01:10 – Cut to the bathroom; she’s now in a steamy shower, water glistening on her skin…”). If lighting changes (she turns off a lamp, or sunset light fades), note it. If there’s a cut or edit in the video (e.g., a jump in time or location), definitely include a timestamp for that transition (“00:30 – Jump cut: now she is outdoors by the pool, drying her hair with a towel…”). However, if the video is one continuous scene (which is common), you may have a continuous timeline with no “cut” notes needed.
	•	Mood and tone via description: Convey how she’s behaving through factual description. Instead of saying “she is feeling shy,” show it: “she glances up through her lashes and covers her chest with her arm, smiling bashfully.” Instead of “she is very horny,” say “she moans loudly and arches her back, moving her fingers faster between her legs.” We want the metadata to reflect mood and intensity by describing actions and expressions.
	•	Keep it third person & descriptive: Do not suddenly switch to second person (“you can see X”) or first person. And do not add commentary like “it’s very sexy” or “this is romantic” – show that through description of the scene (maybe mention “soft candlelight” for romantic mood, etc., as a detail in the description).
	•	Final timestamp: It’s good to include a final timestamp near the end or at the very end of the video (e.g., the last few seconds) to describe how it concludes. For example: “01:55 – She shudders as she climaxes, then collapses back on the pillows with a satisfied grin while catching her breath. 02:00 – Video ends as she blows a kiss to the camera.” This lets the AI know the outcome or resolution of the clip.

Overall, the Long Description should read like a timeline of the video, giving a second-by-second (or at least moment-by-moment) account of everything important that happens. It will likely be several sentences long (much longer than a photo description). That’s expected. Break it into the timestamped lines rather than one giant paragraph, to make it easy to parse. This field is internal, so format it for clarity over beauty.

Explicitness

Purpose: Categorize the video’s sexual/graphic content level so the system knows how adult or risky it is. This classification is crucial for content filtering and appropriate use in chat (e.g., we shouldn’t accidentally show an NSFW video in a SFW context).

Format: Choose one of the controlled values: SFW, Tease, or NSFW (all lowercase when stored, but we often capitalize in writing here for clarity). We also have an “unknown” option, but in practice you should nearly always be able to categorize the video, so avoid “unknown” unless truly no info (which shouldn’t happen with video).

Definitions: Use the most explicit moment in the video as the determinant:
	•	SFW (Safe For Work): No nudity or sexual acts visible. The creator is fully or modestly clothed, and there’s no overt sexual activity. SFW content might be flirty or sexy in theme, but visually would be rated PG-13 at most. Examples: She’s wearing a dress or bikini and posing; she’s casually talking to the camera while clothed; even a passionate dancing video can be SFW if clothes stay on and no explicit touching occurs.
	•	Tease: Suggestive or erotic content without clear full nudity or explicit sex acts. This includes implied nudity (e.g., topless but covered by hands or hair), lingerie (generally at least Tease), see-through fabric that hints but does not clearly show nipples/genitals, or sexual suggestion without a clear explicit view. Examples: She is topless but always covers nipples with her arm/hands; she flashes the camera briefly but at an angle where you can’t clearly see everything; she’s wearing very sheer panties that almost show something; or she’s simulating a sexual act (like hand inside underwear) but not actually showing genitals. Also, if only breasts are fully exposed but nothing else, some categorize that as Tease (though bare nipples often count as NSFW in many definitions—use context: one quick, unclear flash might be Tease, but sustained clear view is NSFW by our rules).
	•	NSFW (Not Safe For Work): Full nudity or explicit sexual content is present. If at any point in the video the creator’s genitals or nipples are clearly visible, or if any actual sexual act (masturbation, use of sex toys, intercourse, oral sex, etc.) is visibly occurring, mark NSFW. Basically, if a frame of the video would be considered pornographic or would get an R/NC-17 rating, it’s NSFW. Examples: She fully strips naked and you can see breasts/nipples and/or genital area clearly; she is actively masturbating with her hand or a toy on camera; any penetration or explicit simulation; two people having sex on camera; etc.
	•	Unknown: Only use this if you truly cannot tell (which in a video scenario is unlikely – you will see what happens). Possibly if content was censored or glitchy. But generally, do not use unknown; classify as SFW/Tease/NSFW accordingly.

How to decide in a video context: Scrub through the whole video mentally (or literally) and identify the most explicit thing shown. Use that to set the category:
	•	If she remains clothed the whole time with only mild sensuality -> SFW.
	•	If she is partially nude or almost showing everything but doesn’t quite cross the line -> Tease.
	•	If you clearly see nudity or a sex act at any point -> NSFW.

When in doubt, err on the side of the higher explicitness. For instance, if it’s borderline (e.g., the lighting is dim but it seems you can see her nipple briefly), treat it as NSFW to be safe. It’s easier to avoid showing NSFW content if mislabeled Tease than vice versa.

Be consistent: this field is not about how sexy or erotic the vibe is, but what is visibly depicted. A video can be extremely erotic in tone but if no actual nudity occurs, it might still be SFW. Conversely, a clinical nude demonstration is NSFW even if not “sexy” per se. So base it on visuals, not on perceived sexiness.

Time of Day

Purpose: Indicates when the scene in the video appears to take place, using a simple classification that is mainly driven by lighting. This helps with continuity and contextual matching – e.g., not sending a clearly daytime clip during a late-night chat unless intended.

Values: Use one of the standard categories:
	•	day – Any clear natural daylight context. This includes morning + midday + afternoon. Use day when the scene has broad, bright, diffuse “daylight” illumination (especially if it appears to come from windows or outdoors). If it looks clearly like daytime, do NOT output anytime.
	•	night – Clear after-dark context. This includes sunset/twilight (“sun falling”) and full night. Use night when outside appears dark (if visible), or when lighting is dominated by artificial sources (lamps/overhead/LED/neon/ring light) and the environment falls into darker corners/shadows, giving an after-dark look.
	•	anytime – Use sparingly. Only when you truly cannot tell day vs night due to missing cues (e.g., windowless/boxed-in rooms like bathrooms, studio/backdrop, tight close-ups with no environment). If the scene is evenly bright and looks like daytime, prefer day over anytime.

How to determine (simple rules):
	•	If windows/outside are visible: bright sky/sunlight → day; dark outside/city lights → night; sunset/twilight glow → night.
	•	If no outside cues: broad natural-looking brightness → day; obvious artificial lighting + darker falloff in corners/background → night.

Location Primary

Purpose: The main setting of the video – a brief noun or phrase for where it takes place. This is the primary location context (e.g., bedroom, kitchen, beach, outdoor patio, etc.). It helps in scenario matching (the AI won’t describe a beach scene as if it were a kitchen).

Format: A single, specific location noun or short phrase. Keep it general but specific enough (e.g., “bedroom,” “bathroom,” “beach,” “car interior,” “gym,” “hotel room,” “swimming pool,” “balcony”). Usually one or two words.

If the video has multiple scenes (e.g., starts in bathroom then moves to bedroom), choose the main or starting location as primary and you can mention the transition in Long Description and possibly add both in Location Tags. But if it’s evenly split, you might pick the one that seems more significant or final. (However, most OnlyFans-style videos are one location.)

Guidelines:
	•	Be specific but not overly narrow: e.g., “bedroom” is good; you don’t need to say “small bedroom” here (that can be in description). “Outdoor pool” or “poolside” is fine; “backyard pool on rooftop” might be too detailed – break it into primary “rooftop” or “poolside” and additional context can go in tags.
	•	If it’s a studio shoot against a backdrop with no real location, you can say “studio” or “indoors” as a fallback (and perhaps add “plain backdrop” in tags).
	•	Only use a location that is evident. If uncertain (like the background is just a wall and you can’t tell if it’s a living room or bedroom), you might default to a general term like “indoors” or “bedroom” if context (like a bed or couch) gives a hint. Try not to leave it blank.

Examples: “bedroom”, “bathroom”, “kitchen”, “living room”, “balcony”, “beach”, “park”, “car”, “shower”, “office desk”, “hotel room”.

Location Tags

Purpose: Additional context tags to further describe or nuance the location/environment. These are like keywords that aren’t covered by the primary location field. They help capture details like setting style, props, or environment specifics that might matter.

Format: A comma-separated list of tags (all lowercase, snake_case format). Each tag should ideally be one word, or two words connected by underscore if needed. This field can be empty if nothing notable beyond the primary location, but usually you can include a couple of environmental details.

Content: Think of elements in the background or setting that set the scene or mood:
	•	Environment type: indoor, outdoor, studio, public, private, urban, nature, etc. (Only if relevant beyond what primary location implies. E.g., primary “balcony” already implies outdoor urban maybe; but you might still tag “night_city” if city lights are visible.)
	•	Notable props or features: bed, sofa, mirror, shower, pool, ocean, car, candles, fairy_lights, etc. Anything the location has that is visually significant. E.g., if in a bathroom with a tub, you might tag “bath” or “bathtub”; in a bedroom with candles, tag “candles”; on a beach, tag “ocean” or “sunset” if visible.
	•	Atmosphere cues: If relevant, tags like “candlelight”, “neon_lights”, “sunset”, “city_lights”, “crowded” (if people around, though OnlyFans likely just her), etc. Be careful not to cross into mood (like “romantic” is more a mood tag unless it’s specifically the setting e.g. “romantic_candles” could be split into candles in location and mood romantic).
	•	Specific sub-locations: If primary is generic like “outdoors,” tags might specify “garden” or “rooftop”. If primary is “bedroom,” tags could note “bed” (if she’s on the bed), “window_view” (if city view out window), etc.

Use 2-5 tags typically, comma-separated, or none if nothing significant. Keep them short (snake_case). For example: bed, candles, night_city for a bedroom with candles lit and city lights outside at night.

Avoid duplicating the primary location. E.g., if primary is “beach”, no need for a tag “sand” unless it adds something (though “sand” could emphasize she’s on the sand vs water). Use tags to enrich, not repeat.

Outfit Category

Purpose: A broad classification of what the creator is wearing (or not wearing) overall in the video. This gives a quick idea of the style of clothing or lack thereof. It’s important for content selection (e.g., user expecting a bikini video vs. a nude video).

Format: A single term from a controlled list of outfit categories:
	•	casual_wear (everyday clothing like jeans, t-shirt, sundress, etc.)
	•	formal_wear (evening gown, cocktail dress, suit, etc.)
	•	lingerie (sexy intimate apparel: bra, panties, garter, corset, etc.)
	•	underwear (if it’s simpler undergarments not really lingerie style)
	•	swimwear (bikini, swimsuit)
	•	nude (wearing nothing essentially)
	•	partial_nude (if only something like a towel or partially clothed in a way that’s not a full outfit)
	•	costume (any roleplay or themed outfit, e.g., maid costume, cosplay, schoolgirl outfit)
	•	athletic_wear (gym clothes, yoga pants, sports bra)
	•	sleepwear (pajamas, nightgown, sleep shirt)
	•	fetish_gear (latex, BDSM harness, etc. if applicable)
	•	other (if truly none of the above apply; use sparingly)

Choose the one that best fits the majority or most notable outfit in the video  .
